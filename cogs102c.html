Role

UX Researcher, Visual Designer

Duration

March 2017 - June 2017

Team Size

6

Tools

Scissors | Tape

Final Design Brief
Overview

In Cognitive Design Studio, teams utilized UX skills and the design process to produce physical solutions for various environmental issues at UC San Diego. My team and I opted to tackle the problem of campus food waste. Our solution was a redesigned disposal bin cover with physical and psychological constraints.

Background

Food waste is one of the most pressing environmental problems facing the globe today. The FAO estimates that about 1/3 of all food produced is wasted, squandering limited resources. Landfill organic waste generates methane, a long-lasting greenhouse gas. At UCSD, students are either unaware or apathetic about this issue.

Challenge

Reduce the amount of post-consumer food waste generated by UCSD that ends up in landfills.

PROCESS
Understanding the User

Interviews

We conducted 30 interviews with UCSD students and relevant organizations, covering a diverse background. Students claimed to have good food waste habits, but also admitted to never separating their scraps and were generally unaware of campus sustainability efforts. Cost and nutritional value appeared to be driving factors behind food purchase decisions.

Observations

We observed users in various dining areas, focusing on the entire meal process from purchase to disposal. Leftovers, portion sizes, and technology use during meals all seemed to affect the amount of generated waste. Food waste receptacles were often contaminated with inorganic waste, and were missing in some campus dining areas.

Target Audience

From this preliminary digging, we found that students are generally indifferent or unaware of the implications of food waste. We focused on UCSD students belonging to the WEIRD (Western Educated Industrial Rich Democratic) demographic, as they are the primary sources of food waste and are generally less affected by the consequences of food waste.

Secondary Research

We simultaneously conducted secondary research on food waste and its existing solutions.

Education Campaigns

Greatest financial benefit to society, but less quantifiable

Sustainability Movements

Inspires passion about issue, but requires constant momentum and funding

Portion Control

Quanitifiable, but may be inconvenient for the user

Major Problems & Needs

We consolidated our primary and secondary research and distilled it into three distinct sections of the food waste issue on campus.

Pre-Consumer

HDH composts food scraps, but only before the food reaches the consumer.

Post-Consumer

Most students do not separate dishware and food scraps correctly after meals.

Infrastructure

Popular dining areas and general disposal bins lack food waste infrastructure.

With a clearer picture of food waste and its implications, we moved on to ideating possible approaches.

Defining Our Route

From these insights, we saw two possible courses of action: preventative (reduction) and reactive (disposal).

  • Preventative: Focus on raising awareness to induce mindfulness
  • Reactive: Focus on reducing post-consumer food waste

From these definitions, a two-pronged approach would be ideal. We highlighted incentive, convenience, and aesthetic as our primary metrics of success to appeal to our target audience. Our solution would have to be generalizable to the entire campus as a whole for maximum impact.

User Testing

We individually pulled three possible solutions from our ideation sessions for closer examination and chose one for prototyping.

  • Food Waste Awareness Campaign
    • Would raise awareness of food waste, but hard to compete for students' attention with other campus issues
  • Color-Coordinated Dishware
    • Decreases cognitive load, but may be expensive for the university
  • General Food Scrap Disposal
    • May alleviate symptom, but must be conveniently located and clearly convey its function

Round 1:
Low-Fidelity Prototype

I prototyped the general food scrap disposal with educational and social aspects. The bin itself would provide a place for food waste while its visuals would generate awareness. The eyes would promote pro-social behavior (i.e. separating out food scraps).

Round 2:
Mid-Fidelity Team Prototypes

We assessed each individual prototype for its strengths and focused on the awareness campaign and disposal bin. I mocked up a poster with a simple visual pun and posted it next to the produce section of an on-campus market. Unfortunately, it attracted little attention despite its novelty.


When we saw that the signs did not work as well as we had hoped, we quickly pivoted and strengthened our more promising prototypes.

Round 3:
Final Team Prototypes

We created two different prototypes of the disposal bin. From our user-testing sessions, we observed that the bin cover produced the best results, while the signs and text were not as noticeable. The holes forced the user to be conscious of their waste and the signs contained relevant examples of waste, customizable for any location.

Finalizing the Product

We built our final prototypes out of plastic bins and cardboard for field-testing, with eyes added to our Food Scrap prototype to harness their psychological effect on behavior.

Field Testing

We placed our prototypes in various locations lacking food waste infrastructure and observed passerby interaction for several hours. Some users bypassed our prototypes and used previously established bins instead, possibly due to a mismatch between the sizes of their scraps and our prototype covers.

Overall, we were quite successful; every piece of waste in our Food Waste bin was organic. Our Trash and Recycle bins also had high degrees of accuracy.

CONCLUSION

From this project, I learned that design is not simply for screens, but universally applicable. Finding a solution is a tenuous balance between feasibility, generalizability, and meaningfulness. Although our design solution may not have been the most groundbreaking, sometimes that old adage holds true: you don't need to reinvent the wheel.
